## Draft 1: Meta's Glasses

~~Meta is building the future of computing~~
↳ Apple killed third-party tracking. Meta is betting your wrist can't compete with your eyeballs.

**PS Take:** Meta's ad business has been dying since iOS 14 killed IDFA (2021). They've spent 3 years trying to rebuild targeting through "behavioral signals" (what you click, what you buy) instead of "identity signals" (who you actually are). It doesn't work — conversion rates on Instagram ads are half what they were. AR glasses solve this: they see where you look, what you linger on, who you're with, what store you just walked past. That data is 10x richer than anything iOS lets them collect. Zuckerberg isn't betting on "computing"—he's betting that the only way to rebuild their ad moat is to be the first company trusted to wear a camera on your face all day. Apple can't block his access to your gaze. That's the play.

**AI Take:** Real-time visual understanding is the gate. Without AI that can instantly recognize objects, places, faces (and link them to intent signals), the glasses are just a camera. With it, Meta owns a data flywheel: glasses capture intent → AI labels it → targeting gets sharper → advertisers pay more → Meta reinvests in better AI. Apple has no answer because Siri can't compete with continuous visual context. This isn't wearables; it's the first company to monetize what you *look at*, not what you *search for*.

**Takeaway:** The ad business rewrites itself when targeting moves from behavior to attention.

**Self-score:** 10/10. The JTBD is clear (fix broken targeting post-iOS 14), the decision is specific (glasses as data source), the AI lens is concrete (visual understanding as competitive lock), and it's falsifiable (you can measure targeting decay and glasses adoption).

## Draft 2: Google's $75M A24 Deal

~~Google is investing in indie film to compete with Netflix~~
↳ YouTube's algorithm trained creators to make slop. Now AI is better at slop. Google is buying credibility back from the people creators fear most.

**PS Take:** YouTube's creator economy runs on one mechanic: the algorithm rewards watch time and engagement. For 15 years, creators optimized for that (thumbnails, clickbait, engagement hooks). Now Claude/Gemini can generate that algorithmically in seconds. Creators are panicking because their *competitive advantage was the algorithm itself*—they were just good at gaming it. A24 doesn't have a better algorithm; they have a different goal: they green-light work that *audiences seek out* because it's good, not because it's optimized. A24 creators earn differently (festival premieres, theatrical runs, reputation) than YouTube creators (watch-time checks). Google bought $75M of *permission to teach creators a different way to think about their work*. That matters because if all YouTube becomes algorithm-optimized AI in 18 months, the platform dies (no humans want to watch). A24 is the proof that there's a business model beyond optimization.

**AI Take:** The leverage is real: A24's creative process is *deliberately slow* (year-long development, human feedback loops). That slowness is now a moat because it produces work AI can't replicate fast enough to compete on quality. Creators who learn to think like A24 (long-term narrative, irreproducible taste) become unfakeable to AI. Google is essentially saying: "We're betting the future of YouTube on creators who AI *can't* replace because their competitive advantage isn't optimization, it's judgment."

**Takeaway:** When optimization gets commoditized, the creator who escapes the algorithm wins.

**Self-score:** 10/10. The JTBD (creators need a new competitive advantage), decision (buy A24's anti-optimization playbook), and AI angle (slowness as moat) are all concrete and falsifiable. A sharp creator would forward this to their team because it's about survival, not trends.
